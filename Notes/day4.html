[00:00:09] 1. Introduction & Recap of Previous Techniques

Welcome to Day 4 of the comprehensive Natural Language Processing (NLP) curriculum. To appreciate the necessity of advanced Deep Learning embeddings, we must first review the classical statistical techniques covered previously.

Classical Text Representation Techniques
  • Bag of Words (BoW): Represents text by counting the frequency of words in a document.
  • TF-IDF (Term Frequency - Inverse Document Frequency): Weighs the frequency of a word by how rare it is across the entire corpus, prioritizing unique keywords.
  • One-Hot Encoding: Represents a word as a binary vector where only the index corresponding to the word is 1, and all other elements are 0.

[00:11:47] 2. The Limitations of Frequency-Based Methods

While classical methods like TF-IDF and BoW serve as excellent baselines, they suffer from critical architectural flaws when applied to large-scale, complex NLP tasks.

Why we need to move beyond BoW and TF-IDF
  1. Inability to Capture Semantic Meaning: Words like "good" and "honest", or "good" and "better" are semantically related. However, BoW/TF-IDF treats them as completely distinct mathematical entities with zero relationship.
  2. The Curse of Dimensionality (Sparse Matrices): If you have a vocabulary of 50,000 words, a single one-hot encoded vector will have 49,999 zeros and a single one. This massive sparsity leads to inefficient memory usage and severe model overfitting.
  3. No Contextual Awareness: Order of words is completely discarded (hence the term "bag" of words).
Analogy

Using One-Hot Encoding is like organizing a library where every single book gets its own completely separate building. It takes up infinite space (high dimensionality) and books on the same subject (semantics) are miles apart.

[00:12:46] 3. What are Word Embeddings?

Definition

Word Embedding is a technique in NLP where individual words are represented as dense, continuous-valued vectors in a predefined vector space. Unlike sparse vectors, embeddings capture the contextual, semantic, and syntactic relationships between words.

The core objective of Word Embeddings is to solve the issues of sparsity and semantic ignorance. Word embedding techniques are broadly classified into two categories:

Key Advantages of Embeddings
  • Fixed, Limited Dimensions: Instead of vocabulary-sized dimensions (e.g., 50,000), embeddings compress representations into fixed dimensions (e.g., 100, 200, or 300).
  • Dense Vectors (Reduced Sparsity): Vectors are filled with float values (e.g., [0.12, -0.45, 0.89...]) rather than zeroes.
  • Semantic Clustering: Words with similar meanings will have vectors placed very close to each other in the multi-dimensional space.

[00:16:03] 4. Feature Representation & Semantic Meaning

To understand how a neural network represents a word, imagine mapping every word across abstract "features." In a real model (like Google's Word2Vec), there are typically 300 dimensions. For human intuition, let's pretend these dimensions map to distinct human concepts.

Vocabulary Word Feature 1: Gender Feature 2: Royal Feature 3: Age Feature 4: Food
Boy -1.00 0.01 -0.50 0.02
Girl +1.00 0.02 -0.50 0.01
King -0.95 +0.96 +0.70 -0.05
Queen +0.95 +0.96 +0.70 -0.05
Apple 0.00 -0.01 +0.95 +0.99
Observation

Notice how the vectors for King and Queen are almost identical across the Royal and Age dimensions, but perfectly opposite on the Gender dimension. This numerical mapping allows mathematical operations on language!

Famous NLP Equation: King - Man + Woman ≈ Queen

By subtracting the "male" concept from "king" and adding the "female" concept, the resulting vector point is mathematically closest to the vector representing "queen".

[00:23:12] 5. Cosine Similarity & Vector Mathematics

Once words are converted into dense vectors, how do we ask the computer: "How similar are these two words?" We use a mathematical concept called Cosine Similarity.

Cosine Similarity Definition

It measures the cosine of the angle (θ) between two multi-dimensional vectors projected in a space. It determines whether two vectors are pointing in roughly the same direction.

  • If Angle = 0° → Cos(0) = 1.0 → Vectors are identical / synonyms.
  • If Angle = 90° → Cos(90) = 0.0 → Vectors are completely unrelated (orthogonal).
  • If Angle = 180° → Cos(180) = -1.0 → Vectors are perfectly opposite / antonyms.
Cosine Distance

Distance is the inverse concept of similarity. To calculate distance mathematically in ML algorithms:

Cosine Distance = 1 - Cosine Similarity

If similarity is 1 (identical), distance is 0.

[00:27:50] 6. Introduction to Word2Vec Architecture

Word2Vec is a predictive deep learning model created by Google. Instead of counting words (like TF-IDF), it trains a shallow Neural Network to predict words. By doing so, it inherently learns the relationships between words. Word2Vec has two distinct algorithmic architectures:

  1. CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words): Predicts a missing target word given the surrounding context words.
  2. Skip-gram: Predicts surrounding context words given a single target word.

[00:28:00] 7. Continuous Bag of Words (CBOW)

Let's dissect exactly how a network learns these weights from scratch using CBOW.

Step 1: Define the Dataset and Window Size

Consider the corpus: "Krish channel is related to data science".

We define a hyperparameter called Window Size. Let's set Window Size = 5 (always prefer odd numbers so there is an equal left and right context around the center word).

Where are the Embeddings?

The magic happens here: We don't actually care about the final predictions of the model once it is trained! The actual Word Embeddings are the Weights learned in the hidden layer.

If our hidden layer has 300 neurons, every word gets mathematically mapped to those 300 weights during forward and backward propagation. This becomes our 300-dimensional vector for that word!

[00:48:33] 8. Skip-Gram Architecture

Skip-gram is literally the inverted architecture of CBOW.

Mechanism

Instead of passing context words to predict the center word, Skip-gram passes the center word as the input and attempts to predict the context words.

Input Data (Target Word) Output Labels (Context Words to Predict)
is [Krish, channel, related, to]
related [channel, is, to, data]
CBOW vs. Skip-gram: Which one to use?
  • CBOW: Faster to train and has better representations for more frequent words. Good for smaller datasets.
  • Skip-gram: Works remarkably well with small amounts of training data, and accurately represents rare words or phrases. Generally preferred for large, massive datasets (like Google News).

[00:52:03] 9. Practical Implementation using Python & Gensim

We use the gensim library in Python to interact with Word2Vec models. Google has provided a pre-trained model trained on over 3 Billion words from Google News datasets, generating 300-dimension vectors.

# Install the library if you haven't
!pip install gensim

import gensim
from gensim.models import Word2Vec, KeyedVectors

# 1. Loading the pre-trained Google News model (~1.5 GB download)
# This contains 300-dimensional embeddings for millions of English words.
path = 'GoogleNews-vectors-negative300.bin'
model = KeyedVectors.load_word2vec_format(path, binary=True)

# 2. View the 300-dimension vector for a specific word
vector_king = model['king']
print(vector_king.shape) # Output: (300,)

# 3. Find Contextually Similar Words (Uses Cosine Similarity under the hood)
similar_words = model.most_similar('cricket')
print(similar_words)
# Output: [('cricketing', 0.81), ('cricketers', 0.79), ('test_cricket', 0.76)...]

# 4. Check similarity between two specific words
sim_score = model.similarity('cricket', 'sports')
print(sim_score) # Output: ~0.4002

# 5. Semantic Vector Mathematics (King - Man + Woman = Queen)
# positive = words to add, negative = words to subtract
result = model.most_similar(positive=['king', 'woman'], negative=['man'], topn=1)
print(result)
# Output: [('queen', 0.7118)]

10. Interview Preparation & FAQ

Q1: If a vocabulary has 500 words, how many dimensions will One-Hot Encoding output?

Expected Answer: 500 dimensions. Every word generates a sparse array equivalent to the total vocabulary size.

Q2: Can Word2Vec be trained from scratch, or must it be pre-trained?

Expected Answer: Both. You can utilize massive pre-trained models like Google News Word2Vec for generic language tasks, or you can train Word2Vec entirely from scratch on a domain-specific corpus (e.g., medical journals) using Gensim.

Q3: What type of neural network is used to train CBOW and Skip-gram?

Expected Answer: A shallow Fully Connected Layer (Artificial Neural Network - ANN). It is not trained using CNNs, RNNs, or LSTMs. The embedding layer is essentially just a dense linear layer.

Q4: What three major problems does Word2Vec solve compared to BoW?

Expected Answer: 1) It captures semantic meaning and relationships. 2) It reduces dimensionality from vocab-size to fixed sizes (e.g., 300). 3) It entirely eliminates sparse matrices by providing continuous, dense float vectors.

11. Final Cheat Sheet & Summary

Quick Revision
  • Word Embedding: Technique converting textual words into dense continuous vectors mapping semantic similarities.
  • Cosine Similarity: Formula evaluating angle between vectors. Closer to 1 = Synonyms. Closer to -1 = Antonyms. Closer to 0 = Unrelated.
  • CBOW: Predicts Target word from Context words. Fast, good for small data.
  • Skip-gram: Predicts Context words from Target word. Slower, highly accurate for rare words and large data.
  • Hidden Layer Size = Vector Dimension: The number of neurons selected for the hidden layer determines the exact size/dimension of your output word embeddings.
  • Gensim Library: The industry-standard Python library for implementing, training, and utilizing Word2Vec architectures.
End of Day 4 Notes. Mastery of Word2Vec is the fundamental stepping stone before advancing into Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and modern Transformer (BERT/GPT) architectures!
